2: THE PROBLEM AND ITS BACKGROUND
3: Introduction
4: The TechnoPath: SEAIT Guide Map and Navigation System is a mobile application-based guide map system designed to display the complete layout of the South East Asian Institute of Technology, Inc. (SEAIT) campus, located at Barangay Crossing Rubber, National Highway, Tupi, South Cotabato, 9505, Philippines. The study provides interactive two-dimensional (2D) map of the campus buildings, including the RST, MST, and JST buildings, the Basic Education building, the gymnasium, canteens, playground, and other campus facilities, with each map containing clearly labeled rooms, offices, and key institutional areas organized by floor level. The study further incorporates a QR code access point posted at the main gate of the SEAIT campus, enabling students, faculty, and visitors to immediately load the campus guide map upon arrival by scanning the code with a mobile device, without requiring prior application installation or knowledge of the application URL. The study operates under a structured role-based administrative backend where the Safety and Security Office manage structural building information, the Program Heads of each department manage department-specific data such as classroom assignments and departmental announcements, and the Dean serves as an oversight authority to monitor and validate major departmental updates. An AI Virtual Guide feature is integrated into the study to deliver conversational navigation assistance, allowing users to describe their intended destination in natural language and receive directional guidance based on stored campus navigation data, thereby supporting independent wayfinding across the entire SEAIT campus grounds and buildings.
5: The current orientation and navigation process at the SEAIT campus is conducted entirely through manual and informal means, with no structured digital wayfinding system in place to assist individuals entering or moving within the campus premises. However, there are printed tarpaulins specifically in each facility. Hence, no clear guide map, posted signage, or digital reference is available at the campus entrance or within the campus grounds to direct new arrivals toward the buildings, classrooms, offices, or facilities they need to locate. Guiding is provided informally through verbal instructions from teachers, staff members, or senior students encountered along the way, and the accuracy of the directions received depends entirely on the personal knowledge and availability of the individual being asked. Informational dissemination regarding facility locations, room assignments, and office designations is performed by physically visiting the relevant offices or by relying on word-of-mouth communication among students and faculty, a process that is inherently prone to outdated information, inconsistency, and miscommunication. When classroom assignments are changed at the beginning of each semester or during the academic year, no centralized announcement platform or updated reference material is distributed to inform students and faculty of the new room designations, leaving individuals to discover the changes only upon arriving at the previously assigned classroom and finding the assignment altered. The absence of a formalized navigation process places the burden of campus orientation entirely on the individual, requiring each student, faculty member, or visitor to independently seek out directions through repeated inquiries, trial-and-error exploration, or reliance on the uncertain availability of knowledgeable campus personnel.
6: The absence of a campus guide map and a centralized digital navigation reference have produced recurring orientation challenges among SEAIT students, faculty, and visitors that directly affect academic punctuality, institutional efficiency, and the overall campus experience. [1] No centralized digital guide exists that presents a clear and visual path from the main gate to the different buildings and facilities of the SEAIT campus, causing delays, missed classes, and unnecessary confusion for individuals who are unfamiliar with the campus layout. [2] No updated digital reference or announcement platform exists to broadcast changes in room labels and classroom assignments when modifications occur each semester, leaving students and faculty without a reliable and current source of room designation information and causing repeated instances of students arriving at incorrect classrooms. [3] New students, transferees, and visitors are unable to locate the offices and inquiry desks within the SEAIT campus buildings when seeking academic assistance, administrative transactions, or institutional inquiries, resulting in repeated misdirection, wasted time, and frustration as individuals are directed from one location to another without a definitive reference. [4] Freshmen, transferees, and visitors frequently become confused and disoriented upon entering the SEAIT campus due to unfamiliarity with the campus buildings, classroom locations, and facility layouts, with no immediately accessible and accurate guide map available to orient new arrivals upon entry. [5] Students, faculty, and visitors frequently hesitate to ask for directions from teachers, staff, or senior students due to social reluctance or the unavailability of campus personnel at the moment of need, and when directions are obtained, the information received is often outdated, incomplete, or inaccurate, which compounds the confusion and delays experienced by the individual seeking guidance.
7: TechnoPath: SEAIT Guide Map and Navigation System delivers immediate and practical benefits to students, faculty, visitors, and the institution as a whole by providing a centralized, accurate, and readily accessible digital navigation reference that eliminates the confusion, delays, and misdirection caused by the absence of a formalized campus wayfinding system. Students and visitors gain the access of a guide map to independently locate classrooms, offices, and facilities without relying on verbal directions, while the institution benefits from a structured, updatable platform that reduces repeated inquiries and ensures that navigational information remains current throughout the academic year. These benefits are realized through five core features: an interactive 2D campus ground layout and building floor maps with clearly labeled rooms and highlighted offices; a QR code access point at the main gate for immediate system access upon arrival; a building room update module that allows the Safety and Security Office administrator to add, modify, and delete room labels and classroom assignments; and an AI Virtual Guide that responds to natural language queries with conversational directional guidance based on stored campus navigation data.
8: Objectives of the Study
9: General Objective
10: The study aims to design and develop the TechnoPath: SEAIT Guide Map and Navigation System for the students, faculty, visitors, and the Safety and Security Office of South East Asian Institute of Technology, Inc. (SEAIT), Barangay Crossing Rubber, National Highway, Tupi, South Cotabato, to provide a centralized, interactive, and digitalized guide map and navigation platform that addresses campus orientation and wayfinding challenges.
11: Special Objectives
12: To be able to display a visual route from the main gate to the different buildings and facilities of SEAIT campus and to show interactive 2D floor maps of the SEAIT buildings with labeled rooms to guide students, faculty, and visitors in locating classrooms, offices, and facilities within the SEAIT campus.
13: To be able to provide a digital platform where SEAIT departmental program head, dean, and Safety and Security Office can make announcements and can add, modify, and delete room labels, office designations, and classroom assignments through the administrative backend for accurate and current student, faculty, and visitor reference.
14: To be able to display labeled building maps that highlight the offices, inquiry desks, and key rooms within the SEAIT campus buildings to help students, faculty, and visitors locate them quickly.
15: To be able to provide a scannable QR code posted at the main gate of the SEAIT campus that allows students, faculty, and visitors to immediately access the TechnoPath Guide Map and Navigation System.
16: To be able to provide an AI-powered virtual assistant that answers location-related questions and gives route directions to help students, faculty, and visitors find classrooms, offices, and facilities independently.
17: Scope and Limitations of the Study
18: Scope of the Study
19: The study is developed to address the campus navigation and orientation challenges experienced by students, faculty, visitors, and the Safety and Security Office of South East Asian Institute of Technology, Inc. (SEAIT), Barangay Crossing Rubber, National Highway, Tupi, South Cotabato. The institution currently lacks a centralized and digitalized campus guide, leaving freshmen, transferees, and visitors without a reliable reference for locating buildings, classrooms, offices, and facilities. The TechnoPath: SEAIT Guide Map and Navigation System was developed to provide an accessible, accurate, and updatable digital guide that serves the entire SEAIT campus grounds and buildings, enabling all user groups to navigate the campus independently and efficiently.
20: Campus Navigation Guide with 2D Building Floor Maps 
21: The study provides an interactive 2D campus ground layout and 2D floor maps of the SEAIT campus buildings. The feature allows users to view and interact the main gate, campus walkways, and the locations of all buildings, and to navigate individual building floors with labeled rooms, highlighted offices, and key facility markers. The navigation guide also displays visual routes from the main gate to the target buildings to assist users who are visiting the campus for the first time.
22: Building Classroom and Room Updates 
23: The study allows authorized department program head and dean and the Safety and Security Office to add, modify, and delete room labels, office designations, and classroom assignments within the digital building maps through the administrative backend. The feature ensures that the navigational information displayed to users remains accurate and reflects current institutional arrangements, particularly when room assignments are changed during the academic year.
24: SEAIT Building Room Maps with Highlighted Offices 
25: The study displays labeled maps of the SEAIT campus buildings with highlighted offices, inquiry desks, and key institutional rooms. The feature allows users to retrieve the location of specific offices within the building floors, enabling faster and more accurate navigation to administrative and academic service areas without requiring verbal directions from campus personnel.
26: QR Code Access Point (Main Gate) 
27: The study provides a scannable QR code posted at the main gate of the SEAIT campus. The feature allows any user — students, faculty, or visitors — to retrieve and load the campus guide map immediately upon arrival by scanning the code with a mobile device, without requiring a prior application installation or knowledge of the URL.
28: AI Chatbot / Virtual Guide
29:  The study provides an AI-powered conversational assistant that responds to location-related queries submitted by users. The feature allows students, faculty, and visitors to describe their destination in natural language and receive a guided route or directional instruction based on the stored campus navigation data, enabling independent wayfinding without requiring assistance from campus personnel.
30: Limitations of the Study
31: The TechnoPath: SEAIT Guide Map and Navigation System is limited to the campus premises of South East Asian Institute of Technology, Inc. (SEAIT) and does not extend to off-campus locations or external facilities. The study operates as a 2D visual map and navigation guide and does not incorporate real-time GPS tracking, augmented reality overlays, or three-dimensional building visualization. Navigation through the AI Virtual Guide feature is limited to responses based on the stored campus map and location data, and the study is unable to provide answers outside the scope of campus-related queries. The building room update module restricts data modification access to authorized administrators only, and the study does not allow public users to add, edit, or delete any campus information. The study does not include real-time event scheduling, emergency alerts, or integration with any external institutional management system. The accuracy of map information is dependent on the timely updating of room labels and office designations by the authorized administrators, and the study does not automatically reflect physical changes to the campus that are not entered through the administrative backend.

(SEAIT). The following are the groups that benefit from the conduct of the study:

The study reduces confusion and prevents late attendance by providing accurate and updated navigation references before and during class days. Students are encouraged to navigate between buildings and campus areas independently, without relying on verbal directions from others.
36: Faculty

Each Program Head is granted department-level administrative access to manage classroom labeling and departmental announcements, while the Dean serves as an oversight authority to monitor and validate major departmental updates and ensure information integrity.
38: Safety and Security Office 
39: The study serves as a digital management tool that allows the Safety and Security Office to oversee structural building information, including room creation, deletion, and layout configuration. The study ensures that students, faculty, and visitors receive accurate and updated navigational information, reducing repeated inquiries and miscommunication at the institutional level. The Safety and Security Office, which manages campus safety and gate security operations, holds the appropriate authority to administer the QR code access point posted at the main gate and to maintain the accuracy of the campus navigation data within the system.
40: Visitors 
41: Visitors to the SEAIT campus are among the primary beneficiaries of the study, as they have no prior familiarity with the campus layout. The QR code access point at the main gate allows visitors to immediately load the guide map upon arrival and independently locate offices, facilities, and key campus areas without requiring assistance from campus personnel. The study reduces the inconvenience of seeking verbal directions and ensures that visitors are guided efficiently throughout the campus.
42: Researchers 
43: The researchers involved in the study gain practical experience in system analysis, design, and implementation within an actual educational environment. The study allows the researchers to apply acquired knowledge in developing a real-world solution that addresses an institutional navigation challenge, particularly for freshmen, transferees, and visitors finding and navigating classrooms and facilities within the SEAIT campus.
44: Future Researchers 
45: The study provides future researchers with a reference model for developing campus navigation and digital mapping systems in different institutions. The study can serve as a foundation for further investigations related to campus digital solutions, user experience in educational systems, and location-based information systems within Philippine academic settings.

65: Definition of Terms
66: SEAIT — South East Asian Institute of Technology, Inc., the educational institution located at Barangay Crossing Rubber, National Highway, Tupi, South Cotabato, 9505, Philippines, where the TechnoPath Guide Map and Navigation System is developed and implemented.
67: Navigation System — The wayfinding component of the TechnoPath study that computes and displays visual routes from a starting point to a target destination within the SEAIT campus, using stored map data and pathfinding algorithms to generate step-by-step directional guidance.
68: Guide Map — A visual reference tool presented within the TechnoPath system that displays the layout of the SEAIT campus grounds and building floors, including labeled rooms, highlighted offices, walkways, and facility markers, to assist users in locating their intended destinations.
69: 2D Map — A two-dimensional visual representation of the SEAIT campus layout used in the study to display buildings, rooms, offices, and facilities in a flat, top-down perspective that allows users to identify and locate specific areas within the campus grounds and building floors.
70: QR Code Access Point — A Quick Response (QR) code posted at the main gate of the SEAIT campus that, when scanned by a mobile device, immediately loads the TechnoPath Guide Map and Navigation System interface, providing instant access to the campus guide map without requiring prior application installation or URL knowledge.
71: Role-Based Access — A system design approach used in the TechnoPath administrative backend that assigns different levels of data management authority to different user roles, including the Safety and Security Office for structural building data, Program Heads for department-specific data, and the Dean for oversight and validation of major updates.
72: Safety and Security Office — The institutional office at South East Asian Institute of Technology, Inc. (SEAIT) responsible for campus safety, gate security operations, and structural building information management within the TechnoPath system, including room creation, deletion, layout configuration, and administration of the QR code access point at the main gate.
73: Classroom Assignment — The designated room or location within a campus building where a specific class section is scheduled to meet, which may change per semester or during the academic year and is updated within the TechnoPath system through the administrative backend.
74: AI Virtual Guide — An artificial intelligence-powered conversational assistant integrated into the TechnoPath system that processes natural language queries submitted by users and generates directional responses based on stored campus navigation data to guide students, faculty, and visitors toward their intended destinations within the SEAIT campus.
75: Wayfinding — The process by which students, faculty, and visitors orient themselves within the SEAIT campus and navigate from one location to another, which the TechnoPath system supports through interactive 2D maps, visual route displays, and AI-powered directional assistance.
76: Administrative Backend — The restricted portion of the TechnoPath system accessible only to authorized administrators, including the Safety and Security Office, Program Heads, and the Dean, through which building structural information, room labels, office designations, and classroom assignments are added, modified, or deleted to maintain accurate campus navigation data.
77: Dijkstra’s Algorithm — A shortest-path computation algorithm implemented in the TechnoPath system to calculate the most efficient navigation route between two locations on the campus map, using stored room coordinates and pathway distance data.
78: CRUD — An acronym for Create, Read, Update, and Delete, representing the four basic operations performed on data within the TechnoPath administrative backend for managing room labels, office designations, classroom assignments, and other campus navigation information.
79: Flutter — An open-source mobile application development framework created by Google, used as the front-end technology of the TechnoPath system to build the cross-platform mobile application interface using the Dart programming language.
80: Flask — A lightweight Python web framework used as the back-end technology of the TechnoPath system to handle the online AI chatbot service, processing user queries and returning AI-generated directional responses when internet connectivity is available.
81: Soft Delete — A data management technique used in the TechnoPath system where deleted records are marked as inactive using a flag rather than being permanently removed from the database, preserving data integrity and allowing administrators to restore previously deleted entries.
82: SQLite — A lightweight, serverless, and self-contained relational database engine used as the local on-device database of the TechnoPath system to store campus navigation data, room information, facility details, FAQ entries, and administrative records without requiring an external database server.
83: Application Programming Interface (API). A set of protocols and definitions that allows two software applications to communicate with each other. In TechnoPath, the online AI chatbot mode uses an external AI API to process user queries and return conversational directional responses when internet connectivity is available.
84: Connectivity Detection. The process by which TechnoPath automatically determines whether the mobile device has an active internet connection. Based on the result of this detection, the AI chatbot switches between online mode—which uses the external AI API—and offline mode—which uses the local FAQ database—to ensure continuous navigation assistance regardless of network availability.
85: Dart. An object-oriented, compiled programming language developed by Google, used as the primary programming language for writing the Flutter mobile application frontend of TechnoPath. Dart enables the development of high-performance, cross-platform mobile applications from a single codebase.
86: Database. An organized, structured collection of data stored electronically and managed by a database engine. In TechnoPath, the SQLite database stores all campus navigation data, room and facility information, FAQ entries, feedback records, and administrative user accounts locally on the mobile device, enabling the system to function without an active internet connection.
87: FAQ (Frequently Asked Questions) Database. A local database stored on the mobile device that contains pre-written question-and-answer pairs covering common campus navigation queries. In TechnoPath, the FAQ database serves as the fallback knowledge source for the AI chatbot when the device does not have an active internet connection, enabling offline navigation assistance through keyword-based matching.
88: Feedback. Information submitted anonymously by users—students, faculty, or visitors—regarding their experience with TechnoPath, including star ratings and written comments about map accuracy, AI chatbot performance, navigation results, or specific rooms and facilities. Feedback records are stored in the database and reviewed by administrators to identify areas for system improvement.
89: Floor Map. A two-dimensional, top-down graphical representation of a single floor within a campus building, displayed within TechnoPath to show the layout of rooms, offices, corridors, and key facilities on that specific floor level. Floor maps in TechnoPath are rendered using SVG assets and include labeled rooms, highlighted offices, and floor-switching controls to enable users to navigate between building levels.
90: Interactive Map. A digital map displayed within TechnoPath that responds to user input, allowing users to tap on buildings or rooms to view details, zoom in and out of the campus layout, switch between building floors, and initiate navigation to a selected destination. The interactive map is the primary interface through which students, faculty, and visitors navigate the SEAIT campus within the system.
91: Keyword Matching. A text-processing technique used in the offline mode of the TechnoPath AI chatbot wherein the user’s submitted query is compared against pre-defined keywords associated with FAQ entries in the local database. The FAQ entry with the highest number of matching keywords is returned as the chatbot’s response when internet access is unavailable.
92: Label. A text annotation displayed on a room, office, corridor, or facility marker within the TechnoPath 2D floor maps and campus ground layout map. Labels identify specific locations by name and room type, enabling users to visually recognize and distinguish individual spaces within the campus buildings and grounds. Room labels are managed by authorized administrators through the Building Classroom and Room Updates module.
93: Layout. The spatial arrangement of rooms, offices, corridors, stairwells, and other physical spaces within a campus building or across the campus grounds, as represented in the TechnoPath 2D floor maps and campus ground layout map. The layout data is stored in the system database and reflects the actual physical configuration of SEAIT’s buildings and campus grounds as maintained by the Safety and Security Office.
94: Module. A self-contained functional component of TechnoPath that performs a specific set of related tasks within the system. Each module—such as the Building Classroom and Room Updates module, the AI chatbot module, the QR code access module, and the administrative backend module—operates as a distinct part of the overall system while integrating with the other modules to deliver the complete campus navigation platform.
95: Natural Language. Ordinary human language as used in everyday conversation, as opposed to formal programming or command-based syntax. TechnoPath’s AI Virtual Guide accepts queries submitted in natural language, allowing users to describe their intended destination in conversational terms rather than requiring specific commands or search codes.
96: Navigation Graph. A mathematical data structure composed of nodes (representing navigable locations such as rooms, corridors, and stairwells) and edges (representing the pathways between those locations with associated distance weights) used by TechnoPath to compute and display campus navigation routes through Dijkstra’s Algorithm.
97: Navigation System. The wayfinding component of TechnoPath that computes and displays visual routes from a starting point to a target destination within the SEAIT campus, using stored map data and the navigation graph to generate step-by-step directional guidance for users.
98: Node. In the context of the TechnoPath navigation graph, a node is a stored data point representing a specific navigable location within the SEAIT campus, such as a room, corridor junction, stairwell, entrance, or building exit. Each node stores X and Y coordinates on the 2D map and is linked to adjacent nodes through navigation edges.
99: Notification. A system-generated alert or informational message delivered within the TechnoPath mobile application to inform users of relevant campus updates, connectivity status changes, facility availability changes, or system maintenance events. Notifications are stored in the database and displayed through the notification bell interface, classified by type and priority level.
100: Offline Mode. The operational state of TechnoPath in which the mobile application functions without an active internet connection, relying exclusively on locally stored data—including the SQLite campus navigation database and the local FAQ database—to provide navigation services and AI chatbot responses. In offline mode, the AI chatbot uses keyword matching against the local FAQ database instead of the external AI API.
101: Offline-First Architecture. A system design approach wherein the application is designed to function fully without an active internet connection by storing all essential data locally on the mobile device. TechnoPath follows an offline-first architecture, storing campus map data, room information, and FAQ entries in a local SQLite database so that navigation features remain available even when the device has no network access.
102: Online Mode. The operational state of TechnoPath in which the mobile application is connected to the internet, enabling the AI chatbot to send user queries to the external AI API through the Python Flask backend and receive AI-generated conversational directional responses. The system automatically switches to online mode when connectivity is detected and reverts to offline mode when connectivity is lost.
103: QR Code (Quick Response Code). A type of two-dimensional matrix barcode that can be scanned by a mobile device camera to instantly retrieve encoded information. In TechnoPath, a QR code is posted at the main gate of the SEAIT campus; when scanned, it loads the TechnoPath campus guide map interface directly on the user’s mobile device without requiring prior application installation or knowledge of the URL.
104: Role-Based Access Control (RBAC). A security and data governance approach used in the TechnoPath administrative backend that assigns specific data management permissions to different user roles. The Safety and Security Office is authorized to manage structural building data; Program Heads are authorized to manage department-specific classroom and announcement data; and the Dean is authorized to oversee and validate major departmental updates. Public users have no data modification access.
105: Route. A computed navigational path displayed within TechnoPath that connects a user’s starting point—such as the main gate or current position—to a selected destination room, office, or facility within the SEAIT campus. Routes are calculated using Dijkstra’s Algorithm applied to the navigation graph and are displayed as a highlighted overlay on the 2D campus ground layout map.
106: Scrum. An Agile project management framework used in the development of TechnoPath that organizes work into fixed-length iterations called Sprints. Each Sprint follows the phases of Planning, Analysis, Design, Implementation, Testing, and Review, allowing the development team to deliver incremental, working system components while continuously gathering and incorporating feedback from stakeholders.
107: SHA-256 (Secure Hash Algorithm 256-bit). A cryptographic hash function used in TechnoPath to secure administrator account passwords within the system database. When an administrator sets or changes a password, it is converted into a fixed-length SHA-256 hash value before being stored, ensuring that plaintext passwords are never directly saved in the database.
108: Soft Delete. A data management technique used in TechnoPath wherein a deleted record is not permanently removed from the database but is instead marked as inactive using a boolean flag (is_deleted). This approach preserves data integrity and audit trail history while hiding the record from the active campus map, and allows authorized administrators to restore previously deactivated entries if needed.
109: Sprint. A fixed-length development iteration within the Scrum framework, typically lasting two to three weeks, during which a defined set of system features is planned, designed, built, tested, and reviewed. TechnoPath was developed across four Sprints, each targeting a specific set of system components.
110: SVG (Scalable Vector Graphics). An XML-based vector image format used in TechnoPath to render the 2D campus ground layout map and building floor maps within the mobile application. SVG assets are stored locally on the device and rendered at any zoom level without loss of quality, making them well-suited for interactive campus map displays.
111: User Acceptance Testing (UAT). A phase of software testing in which representative end-users—in this study, sample students, faculty members, and visitors—interact with the fully developed TechnoPath system and evaluate whether it meets the functional requirements and navigational needs identified during the study. UAT results are used to validate the overall effectiveness and usability of the system prior to institutional deployment.

113: RESEARCH METHODOLOGY AND DESIGN
114: This chapter presents the research methodology, development environment, software engineering model, system development phases, project timeline, functional decomposition, use case analysis, storyboard design, database design, entity relationship diagram, and data dictionary employed in the development of TechnoPath: SEAIT Guide Map and Navigation System.
115: Environment
116: TechnoPath: SEAIT Guide Map and Navigation System was developed and implemented at South East Asian Institute of Technology, Inc. (SEAIT), Barangay Crossing Rubber, National Highway, Tupi, South Cotabato 9505, Philippines. The study was conducted within the SEAIT campus premises, covering the RST, MST, and JST buildings, the Basic Education building, the gymnasium, canteens, playground, and other campus facilities and grounds. System development and testing took place during the Second Semester of Academic Year 2025–2026, with the campus environment serving as both the development context and the deployment location for the mobile application.
117: Software Engineering Methodology
118: The development of TechnoPath: SEAIT Guide Map and Navigation System adheres to the Agile Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC) using the Scrum framework. The Agile methodology was selected because the study requires iterative development, continuous stakeholder feedback, and the flexibility to accommodate requirement changes and panel corrections throughout the development cycle. Stakeholders involved in the iterative review process include the panel of evaluators, the Safety and Security Office, and representative end-users.
119: The Scrum framework organizes the development process into time-boxed iterations called Sprints, each lasting two to three weeks, during which a defined set of features is planned, designed, implemented, tested, and reviewed before proceeding to the next Sprint. The iterative nature of Agile enables the delivery of working system increments at the end of each Sprint, facilitating early detection of issues, continuous improvement, and sustained alignment with stakeholder expectations.

121: System Development Phases
122: The development of TechnoPath: SEAIT Guide Map and Navigation System is organized into four Sprints, each containing the standard Agile phases of Planning, Analysis, Design, Implementation, Testing, and Review.
123: Sprint 1 — Core Foundation and Campus Map (Weeks 1–3)
124: Phase 1: Planning — The researchers define the project scope, identify the cited problems, establish the system requirements, and create the product backlog. The Sprint 1 backlog prioritizes the core campus map display, building floor maps, and room labeling features as the foundational components of the system.
125: Phase 2: Analysis — The researchers analyze the campus layout of SEAIT, gather floor plan data for the RST, MST, JST, and Basic Education buildings, identify all rooms, offices, and facilities to be included in the digital map, and define the data structure for storing room coordinates, building information, and navigation paths.
126: Phase 3: Design — The researchers design the 2D campus ground layout map and building floor map interfaces, create the database schema for buildings, facilities, rooms, and navigation nodes, and design user interface wireframes for the home screen, map display, and room directory.
127: Phase 4: Implementation — The researchers develop the Flutter mobile application shell, implement the interactive 2D campus map display using local SVG assets, build the SQLite database with room and facility data, and implement the building floor map viewer with labeled rooms and highlighted offices.
128: Phase 5: Testing — The researchers conduct unit testing on map rendering, room data retrieval, and floor-switching functionality, and perform initial usability testing with sample users to validate map accuracy and interface clarity.
129: Phase 6: Review — The researchers present the Sprint 1 increment to the thesis adviser and panel evaluators, gather feedback on map accuracy, room labeling, and interface design, and document the corrections and improvements to be addressed in the succeeding Sprint.
130: Sprint 2 — Navigation Engine and QR Code Access (Weeks 4–6)
131: Phase 1: Planning — The researchers define the Sprint 2 backlog, prioritizing campus navigation route computation, visual route overlay on the map, the QR code access point feature, and the campus ground navigation guide from the main gate to the buildings.
132: Phase 2: Analysis — The researchers analyze the pathway connections between buildings, rooms, and campus areas; define the navigation graph (nodes and edges) with distance weights; and determine the QR code content and scanning workflow for the main gate access point.
133: Phase 3: Design — The researchers design the navigation route overlay interface, the step-by-step direction display panel, the QR code generation and scanning workflow, and the visual route from the main gate to each campus building.
134: Phase 4: Implementation — The researchers implement Dijkstra’s shortest-path algorithm in Dart for offline route computation, develop the navigation route overlay on the 2D map, build the QR code access point feature that loads the campus guide map upon scanning, and implement the campus ground navigation guide.
135: Phase 5: Testing — The researchers conduct unit testing on the pathfinding algorithm, validate route accuracy across multiple origin-destination pairs, test the QR code scanning workflow on multiple mobile devices, and perform integration testing between the navigation engine and the campus map display.
136: Phase 6: Review — The researchers present the Sprint 2 increment, gather feedback on navigation accuracy, QR code usability, and route display clarity, and document the refinements for the next Sprint.
137: Sprint 3 — Administrative Backend and Room Updates (Weeks 7–9)
138: Phase 1: Planning — The researchers define the Sprint 3 backlog, prioritizing the role-based administrative backend, the building classroom and room update module, and admin login and session management features.

194: Database Design
195: The database design of TechnoPath: SEAIT Guide Map and Navigation System employs SQLite as the local on-device database engine, managed through the sqflite Flutter package. The database stores all campus navigation data, room and facility information, FAQ entries, feedback records, administrative user accounts, and system configuration locally on the mobile device to support offline-first operation.
196: Figure 3.6. The database design of TechnoPath: SEAIT Guide Map and Navigation System, illustrating the table structure, column definitions, data types, and relationships.
197: The database consists of 21 tables organized into the following functional groups:
198: Campus Structure Tables: buildings, facilities, rooms, facility_images — Store the physical campus layout data, including buildings, facilities within buildings, individual rooms with map coordinates, and facility image galleries.
199: Navigation Tables: navigation_nodes, navigation_edges — Store the navigation graph used by Dijkstra’s algorithm, consisting of navigable points (nodes) and pathway connections (edges) with distance weights.
200: AI and FAQ Tables: faq_entries, ai_chat_logs — Store FAQ question-and-answer pairs for offline AI fallback and log all AI chatbot interactions for performance monitoring.
201: User Interaction Tables: feedback, feedback_flags, search_history, usage_analytics — Store anonymous user feedback and ratings, feedback moderation flags, search query history, and anonymous usage events.
202: Administration Tables: users, admin_audit_log — Store admin user accounts with SHA-256 hashed passwords and track all administrative actions for audit trail purposes.
203: Notification Tables: notifications, notification_types, notification_preferences — Store in-app notifications, notification type configurations, and user notification preference settings.
204: Configuration Tables: device_preferences, map_labels, app_config, connectivity_log — Store device-level user preferences, custom map labels, system-wide configuration settings, and internet connectivity tracking records.
